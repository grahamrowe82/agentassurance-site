Agent Assurance

Trust, control, and accountability for AI-operated businesses

Graham Rowe · Phase Transitions

Agent assurance is the discipline of establishing justified confidence in work performed by AI agents, through controls, audit trails, standards, and accountable human ownership, on behalf of clients who cannot verify that work themselves.

A book, serialized one chapter each Wednesday from 8 July 2026.

Contents

Part I The Problem Agent Assurance Solves

The question of this part is the operator's: what did the afternoon's work take on when it started doing real work? Three chapters answer it in order: the condition itself, the reason nobody can simply check the work, and the way of reading controls that the rest of the book assumes. Nothing here requires a technical background; the position it describes is the reader's own.

Part II Precedent

Part I ended with the operator holding a question: what to do about the systems already running. Before that question can be answered well, a prior one has to be settled, and it belongs to the operator and the practitioner alike: whether the help on offer is real, whether the work behind it lasts, and who actually buys it. That is this part's territory, one chapter of history, one of economics, one of the commercial engagement. An operator deciding whether to buy help, wait for the platforms, or build the discipline in-house needs the same three answers as the person selling it.

Part III The Practice of Agent Assurance

This is the core of the book: the controls, stated as a practitioner's reference. From here the book speaks mostly to the practitioner, whether that is a firm the operator engages or the operator doing the work themselves; the address was always to both, and this is where the second reader steps forward. The four chapters run in the order the thesis prescribes: close what engineering can close (chapters 7 and 8), manage the residual by evidence (chapter 9), and keep both alive across the life of the practice (chapter 10).

Part IV The Profession

The question of this part is what the world can actually see of the risk this book manages. Chapter 11 surveys the state of the record: what is publicly kept about agent incidents, why the record is as thin as it is, and what the thinness means for the firms doing the work.
